A few weeks ago, Evagelos Frigis posted an article in the Bushwick Daily which used census data to support pro-gentrification, white supremacist viewpoints disguising as journalism. The article attempted to use "data" to suggest that gentrification in Bushwick isn't happening. The article was so offensive and riddled with errors the Bushwick Daily Editor had to post an apology. What better way to give your false and dangerous opinions some creditably (and maybe get it published too) than whipping up some quick charts in excel and hope that one one notices...

Undoubtedly, numbers and maps have power. This is in part why I helped start the Bushwick Community Map -- to give anti-gentrification and housing advocates the same numerical & technical tools that real estate has.

Following this post, Anthony wrote a fabulous response that I encourage you to read. However, I imagine that some readers out there might have a lagging thought gathering dust in the back of your head: what about those numbers?

Part of me doesn't want to spend the effort to prove that 'gentrification exists' through data when one could just walk down the street and talk to nearly anyone to arrive at the same conclusion. It should be obvious to anyone who'se spent more than 10 minutes in Bushwick over the past 5 or 10 years the degree of change that has occurred. The census's racial categories are wrought with problems. However, when done right, there are useful patterns that can be drawn from the census that illuminate the drastic changes and the rapid gentrification of Bushwick.

This is first in a series of blog posts that will examine the trends that the census actually shows in Bushwick and North Brooklyn.

In 10 years, Bushwick's white population has tripled

One critical problem with the Bushwick Daily's article is it's reliance on outdated data. It's true that it will be hard to track some aspects of neighborhood change due to the temporal nature of the census. Nonetheless, certain demographics changes from 2000 to 2010 clearly show nascent gentrification trends that are widespread today.

In 10 years, Bushwick's white population triples. In 2000 only 3% of Bushwick is White. By 2010, Bushwick has grown to 8.5% white. Many parts of bushwick are over 15%.

These changes occur in the north part of Bushwick as if spilling over from Williamsburg.

It's true that Bushwick's Hispanic/Latino population grew slightly from around 70,000 to around 73,500 -- but that's way under what one might expect. In New York City the Hispanic/Latino population grew from 2.1 Million to 2.3 Million between 2000 and 2010, an increase of about 8%.
